In the peloton, we talk constantly about compliance—not as an abstract engineering metric, but as the difference between finishing a five-hour stage with fresh legs or completely battered muscles. 2. The Math of Road Resistance

To translate what we feel on the road into numbers, we look at the balance of forces acting against our progress. The physical effort required to keep the bike moving forward is represented by a fundamental equation:

Ptotal=Pgravity+Paero+Prr+PdrivetrainP_{\text{total}} = P_{\text{gravity}} + P_{\text{aero}} + P_{\text{rr}} + P_{\text{drivetrain}}Ptotal​=Pgravity​+Paero​+Prr​+Pdrivetrain​

Here is how we interpret these components in the saddle:

  • $P_{\text{total}}$ is the total physical wattage we must generate to overcome gravity, aerodynamic drag, rolling resistance, and mechanical friction in the chain and bearings.
  • $a_{\text{vibration}}$ represents the root-mean-square acceleration of frame vibration. When triaxial accelerometers measure this, they quantify the harshness we feel through the saddle and handlebars.
  • $\eta_{\text{drafting}}$ represents the drafting efficiency coefficient, showing the exact percentage reduction in CdA when we sit closely behind a teammate.

3. Real-World Adaptation and Field Testing

Seeing how a Case Study translates to actual performance on the road helps us trust our equipment:

  1. Suspension Tracking: On rough mountain descents, linear potentiometers fitted to the fork show engineers exactly how the front end compresses and rebounds, keeping the tire glued to the dirt.
  2. Virtual Elevation Audits: By riding constant-power loops on open roads and applying the Chung method, we can determine our aerodynamic CdA within $\pm 1.5%$ without ever stepping inside a wind tunnel.
  3. Pedal Stroke Alignment: Dynamic force vector tracking on a fit bike lets our fitters adjust cleat positions on the fly, protecting our knees from strain after injury or surgery.
