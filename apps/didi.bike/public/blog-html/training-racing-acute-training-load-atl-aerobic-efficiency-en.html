Understanding Acute Training Load ATL through Aerobic Efficiency

1. Coaching the Aerobic Engine

To build a podium-contending cyclist, you must prioritize structured physiological progression over random mileage. Our main diagnostic target is the athlete's cardiovascular adaptation. By closely supervising the Acute Training Load ATL, we ensure our riders accumulate enough training volume to stimulate adaptation without crossing into chronic exhaustion. Through targeted prescriptions that build Aerobic Efficiency, we instruct athletes to ride faster with less energy expenditure, maximizing their physical capacity.

When we send athletes to altitude camps in St. Moritz or Sierra Nevada, we are deliberately forcing their cardiorespiratory systems to adapt. In these low-oxygen environments, we schedule structured Zone 2 endurance blocks to drive EPO stimulation and expand blood plasma volume. We monitor heart rate and power output daily to prevent metabolic decoupling, preparing their bodies for the final phase of supercompensation before they line up at the start gate.

3. Prescribing Aerobic Efficiency Workouts

As a coach, you should implement these training strategies to refine your riders' physiological profile:

  1. VLaMax Anaerobic Capacity Management: To lower VLaMax and preserve carbohydrate reserves, program low-cadence, high-torque intervals (50-60 RPM) during long endurance rides. This protocol trains the muscles to rely on fat oxidation, keeping glycogen stored for the decisive moves.
  2. Heart Rate Decoupling: Instruct your athletes to watch for drift during four-hour Zone 2 rides. If their heart rate increases by more than 5% while mechanical power remains steady, it indicates their aerobic base needs further development.
  3. W' Reconstitution Dynamics: When designing high-intensity interval training (HIIT), structure the recovery periods based on $W'$ reconstitution rates. Giving athletes enough time between sprints allows their anaerobic energy pool to recharge, ensuring they can hit their power targets in subsequent sets.
