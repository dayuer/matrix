My Race Against the Clock: Living with a Mountain Bike Suspension Potentiometer

Standing at the starting gate of a downhill track, you do not think about physics. You think about traction. Yet, behind every fraction of a second saved on the dirt lies a rigorous verification process. This is the story of how we take raw, high-frequency track telemetry—specifically from a mountain bike suspension potentiometer—and turn it into podium-worthy speed.

On the Dirt: Translating Feel into Hard Numbers

Every rider knows the feeling of a fork that packs down or a rear shock that blows through its travel. Historically, we relied on "feel." Today, teams like the Tudor Pro Cycling squad take a different route. By capturing high-frequency metrics during actual runs, our support crews can map frame vibration dampening and mechanical stresses in real time. We no longer guess where energy is being lost; the data tells us exactly where the bike is fighting the track.

The Math Behind My Muscle Memory

To understand how forces act upon the chassis and rider, our engineering team uses basic physical equations to analyze drag and power distribution. During drafting tests, for instance, we calculate efficiency using:

Where:

  • $P_{\text{total}}$ represents the total mechanical power output required to overcome gravity, aerodynamic drag, rolling resistance, and drivetrain friction.
  • $a_{\text{vibration}}$ is the root-mean-square acceleration of frame vibration, measured via triaxial accelerometers to evaluate dampening characteristics.
  • $\eta_{\text{drafting}}$ represents the drafting efficiency coefficient, representing the percentage reduction in CdA when riding in a slipstream.

Every watt saved by reducing vibration or tweaking our riding position is a watt we can spend pushing harder in the final sprint.

What We Learned on the Race Course

Applying telemetry data to our actual setups has completely shifted how we prepare for race day:

  1. Suspension Telemetry Validation: By mounting linear potentiometers directly on my fork, the mechanics visualised compression and rebound speeds. They tuned the shims to keep my front wheel glued to the dirt on high-speed root sections, vastly improving cornering traction.
  2. Virtual Elevation Field Protocols: Using the Chung method on constant-power loops, we calculated our aerodynamic CdA to a precise $\pm 1.5%$ margin. It gave us wind-tunnel-level accuracy on a budget.
  3. Pedal Stroke Alignment: Tracking the pedal force vectors on a fit bike allowed us to adjust my cleat position. This minor change eliminated the nagging knee strain that usually flares up after heavy training blocks.
