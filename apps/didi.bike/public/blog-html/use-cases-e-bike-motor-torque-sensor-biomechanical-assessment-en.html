Human-Machine Coupling: Quantifying Musculoskeletal Efficiency through Biomechanical Assessment of E-Bike Motor Torque Sensors

1. Kinematic Modeling and Methodology of Biomechanical Assessment

In human-machine cycling systems, the mechanical output of the lower limbs and the distribution of joint strain represent fundamental areas of study in sports science. This paper discusses the application of systematic Biomechanical Assessment protocols to analyze the output of the E-Bike Motor Torque Sensor alongside high-frequency telemetry. By capturing multi-axis force signals, we map how pedal reaction forces, joint kinematics, and road vibrations affect musculoskeletal efficiency.

For example, during investigations into the aerodynamics of group formations with Swiss-based Tudor Pro Cycling, researchers examined the biomechanical impact of drafting. Using dual-sensor wind speed telemetry, they tracked how changing pressure fields within a team formation alter the muscular recruitment patterns of riders. This Biomechanical Assessment combines fluid dynamics with joint kinematic data to build models that inform pacing strategies based on physiological efficiency.

2. Work-Energy Conservation and Lower Limb Muscular Loading

To model the mechanical work required by the system, we express the total power output using the classical energy conservation equation:

Ptotal=Pgravity+Paero+Prr+PdrivetrainP_{\text{total}} = P_{\text{gravity}} + P_{\text{aero}} + P_{\text{rr}} + P_{\text{drivetrain}}Ptotal​=Pgravity​+Paero​+Prr​+Pdrivetrain​

Within a biomechanical framework, these parameters map to musculoskeletal recruitment as follows:

  • $P_{\text{total}}$ is the net mechanical power generated by the lower limbs, primarily through hip extension (gluteus maximus) and knee extension (quadriceps femoris).
  • $a_{\text{vibration}}$ represents the root-mean-square acceleration of the frame. Higher values increase isometric muscle contractions required to stabilize the body, accelerating local muscular fatigue.
  • $\eta_{\text{drafting}}$ represents the drafting efficiency coefficient, which directly correlates with the reduction in metabolic energy expenditure when riding in a low-pressure wake.

3. Practical Applications of Biomechanical Assessment in the Field

Our research applies structured Biomechanical Assessment to three key areas of performance optimization:

  1. Suspension Compliance and Joint Loading: By monitoring fork travel with linear potentiometers on gravel surfaces, we evaluate how different damping configurations absorb high-frequency road shock, reducing impact transmission to the patellofemoral joint and lumbar spine.
  2. Outdoor Aerodynamic Profiling via Chung Protocol: Using constant-power loops on outdoor tracks, we calculate the aerodynamic CdA with $\pm 1.5%$ precision. This allows researchers to adjust the rider's position to reduce drag without restricting joint range of motion or respiratory capacity.
  3. Pedal Stroke Optimization via Inverse Dynamics: We measure orthogonal force components during the pedal stroke. For athletes returning from knee surgery, this helps fitters adjust cleat alignment to balance load distribution and prevent joint stress.
