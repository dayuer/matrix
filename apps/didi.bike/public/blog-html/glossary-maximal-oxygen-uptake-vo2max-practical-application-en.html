Prescribing the Interval Block: Practical Application of Maximal Oxygen Uptake VO2max

To build a world-class aerobic engine, you cannot rely on slow, steady miles alone. You must push your body to its absolute limits. In elite cycling coaching, the practical application of maximal oxygen uptake vo2max data involves designing specific, high-intensity interval prescriptions. By training at or near your maximum oxygen intake, you trigger cellular training adaptations that expand your cardiovascular capacity, allowing you to sustain higher target wattages for longer durations.

When you execute a VO2max workout, you are operating in Zone 5 (106-120% of your Functional Threshold Power). These efforts are extremely demanding. They place a massive stress on your cardiovascular system, forcing your heart to pump blood at its maximum stroke volume. Physiologically, these intervals deplete your muscle glycogen rapidly. To manage this fatigue accumulation, coaches must balance these hard efforts with active recovery, optimizing your lactate clearance capacity between blocks.

To monitor the metabolic cost of these workouts, we analyze the relationship between mechanical power and cardiorespiratory strain. We calculate your Normalized Power (NP) to account for the physical stress of these high-intensity intervals:

Where:

  • $\text{TSS}$ and $\text{NP}$ reflect the exponential weighting of training stress, scaling with the 4th power of mechanical power output to match physiological load.
  • $RER$ represents the Respiratory Exchange Ratio, indicating substrate oxidation ratios (carbohydrate vs. fat combustion).
  • $W'_{t}$ represents the instantaneous anaerobic work capacity remaining, measured in Joules (J), which drains non-linearly above FTP and reconstitutes exponentially during recovery.

From a practical perspective, understanding your VO2max limits allows us to customize your interval prescription. If you have a high anaerobic capacity but a lower aerobic ceiling, your $W'$ tank will drain quickly above FTP, leading to rapid exhaustion. By focusing on VO2max block training, we stimulate training adaptations that lift your critical power. This expansion increases your glycogen sparing capacity at submaximal speeds, keeping your RER low and preserving your anaerobic matches for the final kilometers of a race.

Peak Aero Engine Week 3 5 min reps 110-115% FTP 90-95 RPM 5 min easy

Your training execution checklist during these sessions:

  1. Maintain High Cadence: Keep your spin above 95 RPM to shift the load from your muscles to your cardiovascular system.
  2. Focus on Breathing: Ensure deep, rhythmic inhalation to maximize oxygen delivery to the blood.
  3. Manage Recovery: Keep pedaling slowly during the recovery intervals to accelerate lactate clearance.

By applying these training principles, you will expand your cardiovascular capacity. The objective data from your power meter will prove your adaptations, showing higher sustainable wattages and faster recovery rates across all training zones.
