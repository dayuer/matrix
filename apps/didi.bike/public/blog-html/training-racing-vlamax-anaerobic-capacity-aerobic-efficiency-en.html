Understanding VLaMax Anaerobic Capacity through Aerobic Efficiency

1. Coach's Log: Balancing Lactate and Endurance in Mallorca

During our spring training camp in Mallorca, I tested two riders with identical FTP numbers on paper. However, three hours into a steady endurance ride, one rider was completely depleted while the other was still riding comfortably. The difference was VLaMax Anaerobic Capacity. The depleted rider had a high anaerobic capacity, meaning their body default-burned carbohydrates at a rate their digestive system could not keep up with. As a coach, developing aerobic efficiency is about building the aerobic base while keeping VLaMax low enough to protect glycogen stores during long-distance races.

By monitoring the feedback loops of these metabolic rates, we can adjust weekly volumes and intensity to ensure athletes build the endurance needed for stage racing.

2. Measuring the Athlete's Aerobic Gas Exchange

To assess how effectively an athlete uses oxygen to fuel their effort and clear metabolic waste, we track gas exchange kinetics:

VO2=VE⋅(FIO2−FEO2)VO_2 = V_E \cdot (F_I O_2 - F_E O_2)VO2​=VE​⋅(FI​O2​−FE​O2​)

Where:

  • $VO_2$ represents the systemic rate of oxygen consumption, the key metric I use to gauge a rider's cardiorespiratory engine capacity.
  • $V_E$ is the minute ventilation volume, measuring how much air the lungs move per minute.
  • $F_I O_2$ and $F_E O_2$ represent the fraction of oxygen in the inspired and expired air, showing the efficiency of oxygen extraction at the alveoli.

Evaluating these parameters during step tests helps me structure accurate recovery blocks and training intensities for each rider.

3. Practical Coaching Interventions for the Endurance Engine

Here are the protocols I write into our team's daily training logs:

  1. Low-Cadence Torque Intervals: I prescribe 3 sets of 15 minutes at 50-60 rpm on sustained climbs. This low-cadence work targets fast-twitch muscle fibers, forcing them to adapt aerobically and suppressing the VLaMax valve to improve fat oxidation.
  2. Tracking Cardiac Decoupling: During 4-hour endurance rides, I look at the decoupling ratio between heart rate and power. If a rider's heart rate drifts upward by more than 8% while power stays constant, it indicates a drop in aerobic efficiency and a need to adjust hydration.
  3. Managing the Anaerobic Pool ($W'$): During group sessions, I monitor the live telemetry of our riders' $W'$ anaerobic capacity. I instruct them to sit in and ride at a steady tempo when their $W'$ falls below 30%, letting the battery recharge before their next acceleration.
