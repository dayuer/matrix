Behind the Bars: Reaching for Comfort and Injury Prevention on Long Stages

Where the Rubber Meets the Road

After racing six hours in the pouring rain during the Transcontinental, I realized that a few millimeters of reach can dictate whether you finish the stage or abandon due to knee strain. When my cockpit was stretched too far, I spent days fighting the machine rather than riding it. Every bump in the asphalt sent shocks directly up my arms. Pain is real. The road punishes. My back was screaming.

Holding 50 km/h in the breaks requires total efficiency. Yet, my body was constantly sliding forward to compensate for the excessive reach. This displacement altered my hip angle. The resulting misalignment caused a dull ache in my left patellar tendon. To solve this, we had to analyze the kinematics.

From the Saddle to the Lab

We ran dynamic diagnostics to verify my joint angles. We found that shortening my cockpit component by fifteen millimeters allowed my pelvis to rotate backward. The road vibration felt less jarring. I felt relief. My speed increased.

Our team recorded saddle pressure during the next qualifying split:

  1. Pressure Balance: The center of pressure shifted back, resolving numbness.
  2. Screaming Muscles Silenced: Gluteal activation rose by twelve percent.
  3. Lateral Tracking: Varus movement of the right knee dropped below three millimeters.

In the end, preventing injury requires balancing cockpit dimensions with your skeleton's limits.
