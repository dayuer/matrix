Understanding Lactate Threshold LT1 and LT2 through Intensity Optimization

1. Portfolio Optimization of Training Intensities

From a product management perspective, an athlete's physical capacity is a resource allocation problem with a strictly limited budget of weekly recovery time. Lactate Threshold LT1 and LT2 function as critical inflection points defining the risk-reward profile of different training zones. By applying Intensity Optimization workflows, coaches manage training resources, defining performance outcomes against fatigue accumulation. LT1 represents the entry-level baseline—a low-cost zone with a high return on investment (ROI) for mitochondrial density. LT2 is the high-cost threshold where performance margins shrink, demanding high recovery overhead to manage the metabolic debt.

During altitude blocks in St. Moritz or Sierra Nevada, tracking this portfolio's performance helps sports scientists evaluate biological assets. They measure plasma volume expansion and metabolic decoupling to calculate adaptation trends, ensuring the training asset reaches its peak valuation on race day.

3. Product Feature Roadmaps and Resource Allocation

Deploying Intensity Optimization strategies within a training program enables teams to maximize physiological returns:

  1. Managing VLaMax to Optimize Glycogen Burn Rates: Adjusting the anaerobic capacity metric (VLaMax) via low-cadence torque blocks acts as an optimization feature. Tuning this limit controls carbohydrate combustion rates, preserving critical glycogen resources for the final stage sprint.
  2. Heart Rate Decoupling as an Efficiency Metric: Monitoring the separation between heart rate and mechanical power during long rides identifies early signs of resource depreciation (cardiac drift). This functions as a diagnostic tool for aerobic efficiency and cardiovascular fatigue.
  3. Pacing Optimization via W' Reconstitution Dynamics: Real-time modeling of $W'$ depletion acts as a dynamic budget calculator for anaerobic efforts. This allows team directors to map out precise climbing strategies, preventing riders from overspending their limited anaerobic energy reserves.
