Understanding Lactate Threshold LT1 and LT2 through Aerobic Efficiency

1. Laying the Base: Build Aerobic Capacity First

Listen up: if you want to survive the final climbs in a major race, you must stop training by raw feeling and start structuring your zones. Lactate Threshold LT1 and LT2 are your roadmap. As your coach, my job is to push these numbers up so you can ride faster while burning less fuel. Think of LT1 as your all-day tempo—the intensity where we build your engine. LT2 is your threshold redline. We training this to make sure you can hold high power on climbs without blowing up.

When we head to altitude training camps in St. Moritz or Sierra Nevada, we are there to force adaptation. We monitor your recovery closely, using blood markers and heart rate metrics to calculate your plasma expansion and metabolic decoupling. This ensures you hit peak supercompensation exactly when the race begins.

2. Tracking the Work: How We Measure Training Load

To monitor your fatigue and fitness, we calculate your load daily. Forget guesswork; we use mathematical formulas to track how your body responds to training around Lactate Threshold LT1 and LT2:

Here is how we break down these metrics:

  • $\text{CTL}_t$ and $\text{ATL}_t$ represent your Chronic and Acute Training Load. We calculate these using exponential decay time constants of 42 days and 7 days.
  • $\text{TSB}_t$ is your Training Stress Balance. We use this to predict when your body is fresh and ready to race as the balance goes positive.
  • $VO_2$ represents the oxygen consumption rate, calculated as a function of ventilation volumes ($V_E$) and oxygen concentration differentials.

3. The Workouts: How We Train Aerobic Efficiency

Here is how we put this data into action. You will execute these three target workouts to build your metabolic efficiency:

  1. Low-Cadence Torque Intervals for VLaMax Control: Ride 3x15 minutes at sweet spot (just below LT2) with a cadence of 50-60 RPM. Focus on keeping your upper body perfectly still. Grinding these torque blocks lowers your VLaMax, saving glycogen reserves for the final sprint.
  2. Endurance Rides to Limit Decoupling: Schedule a 4-hour zone 2 endurance block (keep your power strictly below LT1). Keep your posture relaxed. We will monitor the separation between your heart rate and power to track cardiac drift.
  3. Over-Under Intervals for W' Recovery: Perform 4x10 minutes of over-unders: 2 minutes at 105% of LT2, followed by 2 minutes at 90% of LT2. This teaches your body to clear metabolic waste under load, training your $W'$ battery to recharge faster.
