Understanding Lactate Threshold LT1 and LT2 through Fatigue Management

1. Riding the Edge: The Reality of Metabolic Thresholds

When you are five hours into a mountain stage, you do not think about theoretical training zones; you feel them in your lungs and the deep ache in your quadriceps. Lactate Threshold LT1 and LT2 are not just lines on a computer screen. They represent the barrier between a sustainable rhythm and absolute failure. To us, LT1 is that familiar, steady tempo where we can still talk, but we feel the glycogen burn starting to build. LT2 is the redline. Push a single watt above it, and the clock starts ticking. Your throat tastes like copper, sweat drops onto your top tube, and you know you only have minutes before your legs lock up.

During our three-week altitude camps in St. Moritz or the Sierra Nevada, the air is thin, and every breath feels like drawing from an empty glass. We monitor our recovery closely, tracking how our bodies adapt to hypoxic stress through plasma volume expansion and metabolic decoupling to ensure we have that extra gear when the race hits the final climbs.

2. Pushing the Limits: The Internal Metrics of Work

We use data to measure what our bodies are experiencing on the road. By tracking how we respond to efforts around Lactate Threshold LT1 and LT2, we monitor our physiological limits:

3. Surviving the Long Stages: On-Bike Energy Management

Translating these fatigue metrics into our pacing strategy is what keeps us in the front group when the hammer drops:

  1. Controlling the Burn through VLaMax: Adjusting our anaerobic engine via grinding low-cadence blocks or violent sprints shapes how we burn fuel. Keeping our VLaMax in check prevents us from burning through glycogen too early, preserving that reserve for the final dash to the line.
  2. Battling Cardiac Drift: During long, hot days, we watch our heart rate creep upward even when our power meter shows a steady state. This metabolic decoupling tells us that our cardiovascular system is working overtime to keep us cool, reminding us to drink and ease off the pace.
  3. Reconstituting the W' Battery: Going deep into the red depletes our $W'$ tank. Knowing how fast we recover when we drop back below LT2 allows us to time our attacks and manage our efforts over successive climbs.
