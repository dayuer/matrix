Unlock Your Aerobic Engine with Targeted High-Intensity Workouts

If you want to win bike races, you cannot rely on luck. You must build a bigger engine. In my twenty years of coaching athletes at the WorldTour and Olympic levels, I have learned that the maximal aerobic power map is the single most important diagnostic tool in your training arsenal. This metric measures your capacity to produce power at the absolute limit of your aerobic energy system. If you want to drop your rivals on the final climb, you need to lift this ceiling. Let's look at how we design precise training sessions to force the body to adapt. Push your limits.

Prescribing the Interval Block

To trigger positive training adaptations, you must subject your body to structured overload. We do not just go out and ride hard. We target specific metabolic pathways using precise target wattage zones. To expand your aerobic engine, I prescribe high-intensity intervals that force you to work at one hundred and fifteen to one hundred and twenty percent of your functional threshold power.

When you execute a high-intensity interval prescription on a steep climb, your body undergoes massive cardiovascular adaptations to deliver oxygen to your working muscles. Your stroke volume increases, your capillary density expands, and your mitochondria multiply. These changes allow your muscles to produce more energy using oxygen, delaying the point where you must rely on burning scarce glycogen stores. Glycogen sparing is the key to surviving long, brutal road races. Focus on cadence.

The Mechanics of Fatigue Accumulation

During intense intervals, metabolic waste products accumulate in your muscle fibers. You feel this as a deep burn. Your lactate clearance capacity determines how quickly you can recover from these efforts. If you have a well-developed aerobic engine, you can clear these waste products rapidly, even during active recovery phases. This allows you to back up hard efforts multiple times.

Structuring the Metabolic Zones

You must balance high-intensity sessions with structured endurance work to build a robust foundation. I structure training into seven distinct zones, ranging from active recovery up to neuromuscular power. If you spend too much time riding in no-man's-land—too hard for recovery, too easy for adaptation—you will accumulate fatigue without driving performance gains.

We use sweet spot intervals to build aerobic fatigue resistance. This zone sits just below your threshold, offering a high training stimulus with relatively low fatigue accumulation. By combining sweet spot sessions with focused VO2max blocks, we optimize your physiological adaptations over a multi-month periodization cycle. Listen to your body. Hydration is required.

Your Training Execution Checklist

To get the most out of your training, you must execute every session with clinical precision. Follow this simple checklist before every interval block:

  1. Verify Calibration: Perform a zero-offset calibration on your power meter to ensure your target wattage is accurate.
  2. Select the Terrain: Find a steady climb with a consistent gradient of five to eight percent, free of traffic stops.
  3. Monitor Cadence: Keep your spin above ninety-five revolutions per minute to shift the load from your leg muscles to your cardiovascular system.
  4. Fuel Correctly: Consume seventy to ninety grams of carbohydrates per hour to maintain glycogen availability.

By following this disciplined approach, you turn training from a guessing game into a science. You will see your maximal aerobic power map limits expand, giving you the horsepower needed to conquer your racing goals. The work is hard, but the rewards are real.
