Dual-Sided vs Single-Sided Power Meters

A dual-sided power meter measures the force each leg produces independently and reports true total power plus left-right balance. A single-sided meter measures only one side — usually the left — and doubles that value to estimate total output. The choice between them comes down to how much accuracy, balance insight, and budget you need. For most riders, the decision rests on one number: how far your natural leg balance sits from 50/50, a topic tied closely to left-right power balance.

How Each Type Measures Power

Single-sided estimation

A single-sided system installs on one crank arm or one pedal. The strain gauges inside measure torque and angular velocity on that side only, then apply the formula:

Ptotal=2⋅Pmeasured sideP_{\text{total}} = 2 \cdot P_{\text{measured side}}Ptotal​=2⋅Pmeasured side​

This assumes your legs contribute equally. For a rider with a true 50/50 split, the estimate is essentially exact in steady-state riding. But almost no one is perfectly balanced — natural asymmetries of 48/52 to 53/47 are common — so the doubled figure carries a built-in error.

Dual-sided true measurement

A dual-sided (dual-leg) meter has gauges on both the left and right crank or pedal:

Ptotal=Pleft+PrightP_{\text{total}} = P_{\text{left}} + P_{\text{right}}Ptotal​=Pleft​+Pright​

Because each side is measured, total power is accurate regardless of your balance, and the meter can report the percentage split between legs.

The error from a single-sided meter is systematic, not random — it consistently under- or over-reads by the same percentage for a given rider. This means it is internally consistent for tracking your own progress, but absolute watts and any comparison to a differently-sided meter are unreliable. Left-Right Balance: Why It Matters

Dual-sided data reveals your pedaling balance, typically displayed as 50/50 when even. Tracking this over time is useful for:

  • Injury rehab: a recovering leg often drops several percentage points, and you can watch it return to baseline.
  • Correcting asymmetry: sustained imbalances above 53/47 can indicate a fit issue, leg-length difference, or muscle weakness.
  • Fatigue insight: as you tire, the dominant leg often takes a larger share, visible as balance drift late in a ride.

None of this is available from a single-sided meter. If balance work is part of your goals, dual-sided is the only option. See the broader cycling power pedaling guide for how balance fits into the full power-training picture.

Which Should You Buy?

Choose single-sided if

  • Your budget is constrained and you want the core benefit of power training (structured zones, tracking fitness).
  • You train in power zones where ±5%\%% error does not change the zone you target.
  • You have no history of leg injury or known asymmetry.

Choose dual-sided if

  • You are recovering from a leg injury and need to monitor balance restoration.
  • You race and want the most accurate absolute watts for normalized power and performance management.
  • You are a coach or data-driven rider who acts on balance and torque-effectiveness data.

Accuracy Calibration Considerations

Regardless of sides, every power meter needs periodic accuracy calibration to account for temperature drift and zero-offset changes. Dual-sided meters require zeroing each side, which is a minor extra step but ensures both halves read true. Single-sided meters only zero one channel but inherit the balance-assumption error regardless of calibration quality.

FAQ

What is the difference between a dual-sided and a single-sided power meter? A single-sided power meter measures force on one crank or pedal and doubles it to estimate total power. A dual-sided (dual-leg) meter measures both sides independently, reporting true total power plus left-right balance.

How inaccurate is a single-sided power meter? If your left-right balance differs from the assumed 50/50 split, a single-sided meter can read off by 5–10%. For riders with a 48/52 imbalance, every watt is undercounted by about 4%.

Do I need a dual-sided power meter for training with zones? For most structured zone training, a single-sided meter is adequate because zones are wide bands and small errors do not change the zone you land in. Dual-sided is valuable for rehab, balance work, and elite precision.

Is a dual-sided power meter worth the extra cost? If you need left-right data for injury recovery, want the most accurate total power, or race at an elite level, yes. Otherwise a quality single-sided or pedal-based system gives most of the training benefit at lower cost.
