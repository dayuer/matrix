# Deciphering Telemetry: A Data-Driven Study of the Mountain Bike Suspension Potentiometer

Raw sensor outputs are inherently noisy. In the context of high-frequency cycling telemetry, the primary objective is to separate physical phenomena from mechanical vibration. When analyzing datasets generated by a mountain bike suspension potentiometer, we apply statistical filtering and digital signal processing to extract actionable performance trends. 

## Data Extraction: Filtering Out the Field Noise
Field data collected from professional teams like Tudor Pro Cycling presents massive variability. A rider descending a track produces overlapping signals from body weight shifts, pedal inputs, and terrain impacts. To make sense of these datasets, analysts must filter out sensor artifacts. By applying a bandpass filter to the raw telemetry, we isolate structural frame displacement from low-frequency rider movements, revealing the true operational profile of the suspension.

## Quantifying Frame Energy Losses through Vibration Metrics
To evaluate the damping performance of the chassis, we calculate the root-mean-square (RMS) acceleration. This statistic acts as a proxy for mechanical energy lost to the frame:

$$ a_{\text{vibration}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T} \int_0^T [a(t)]^2 \, dt} $$

Where:
*   $P_{\text{total}}$ represents the total mechanical power output required to overcome gravity, aerodynamic drag, rolling resistance, and drivetrain friction.
*   $a_{\text{vibration}}$ is the root-mean-square acceleration of frame vibration, measured via triaxial accelerometers to evaluate dampening characteristics.
*   $\eta_{\text{drafting}}$ represents the drafting efficiency coefficient, representing the percentage reduction in CdA when riding in a slipstream.

By tracking this integral over discrete time window $T$, we assign a numerical score to the ride quality, allowing us to compare different frame designs and shock setups objectively.

## Data-Backed Case Applications
Analyzing field datasets reveals distinct optimization opportunities:
1.  **Suspension Telemetry Validation**: By constructing velocity histograms from linear potentiometer displacement, we calculate the percentage of travel spent in high-speed versus low-speed damping ranges. This guides the re-valving of the shock.
2.  **Chung Virtual Elevation Field Protocols**: Using regression models on constant-power loops allows us to calculate CdA within a narrow $\pm 1.5\%$ margin of error, bypassing the need for expensive wind tunnel facilities.
3.  **Pedal Stroke Optimization**: Mapping pedal force vectors dynamically over the full 360-degree crank rotation identifies torque dead spots, helping fitters reposition cleats to alleviate post-operative joint strain.
