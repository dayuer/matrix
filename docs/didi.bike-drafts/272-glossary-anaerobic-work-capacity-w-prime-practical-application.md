# Calibration of Telemetry for W-Prime Accuracy

## Step 1: Drivetrain Tolerances and Torque Tolerance
Mounting a high-precision power meter requires strict adherence to assembly clearances. To calculate anaerobic work capacity w-prime reliably, the mechanical power readings must be clean. 
First, clean the bottom bracket shell completely. Dirt ruins data. 
Any carbon burrs or metal filings inside the threads will ruin the calibration offset. Apply a thin layer of high-grade marine grease. 
Ensure the bottom bracket cups are threaded by hand to avoid cross-threading. 
Use a calibrated digital torque wrench to secure the components. Torque tolerance is extremely tight. 
We must check for lateral play after tightening. Bottom bracket play or slop detection is completed by pulling the crank arms perpendicular to the frame. No slop is allowed.
If play is detected, insert micro-spacers to take up the axial clearance. Bolts must be snug.
Once the bottom bracket is secured, mount the crankset. Thread lock compound should be applied to the chainring bolts. This prevents vibration looseness during rough gravel stages.

## Step 2: Strain Gauge Centering and Offset Calibration
Mechanical torque applied to the pedals is measured via surface-mounted strain gauges. Strain gauge centering is paramount for accurate bilateral power distribution. 
To isolate friction losses, the chain must be dropped from the chainring during offset zeroing. 
We execute a manual calibration offset process. Rotate the crank arm to the vertical position. 
Ensure there is no external load acting on either pedal. Connect the telemetry unit to the logging console. 
Record the baseline voltage value. 
This offset value must be stored in the non-volatile memory of the device. 
Calibrate the offset.
If the temperature shifts by more than five degrees Celsius, the calibration offset must be re-run to account for thermal expansion of the aluminum crank arm. 
The table below specifies the required tools and torque tolerances for mounting drivetrain sensors:

| Drivetrain Component | Required Tool | Recommended Torque (Nm) | Tolerance (Nm) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Crankset Bolt | Torque Wrench (10mm Hex) | 45 | +/- 2.0 |
| Chainring Bolts | Torx T30 Wrench | 12 | +/- 0.5 |
| Bottom Bracket Cup | BB Spline Socket | 40 | +/- 3.0 |

Play must be zero. 
Grease the threads.

## Step 3: Mechanical Torque Verification
To verify the accuracy of the strain gauges under static loads, we perform weight-hang tests. The mechanical torque generated by a suspended mass is compared against the telemetry output.
The mechanical torque equation is modeled as:

$$ \tau = F \cdot r \cdot \sin(\theta) $$

Where:
*   $\tau$ represents the mechanical torque in Newton-meters (Nm).
*   $F$ represents the gravitational force applied by the calibrated mass in Newtons (N).
*   $r$ is the length of the crank arm in meters (m).
*   $\theta$ is the crank angle relative to the gravity vector.

We measure torque.
During the verification protocol, a certified twenty-kilogram mass is suspended from the pedal spindle at a ninety-degree angle ($\theta = 90^\circ$).
This yields the maximum torque output. 
The telemetry console displays the calculated torque.
Compare the values.
The error margin must remain below one percent to ensure reliable calculations of anaerobic work capacity w-prime. 
Once static calibration is complete, the normalized power ($\text{NP}$) mathematical relation is used during real-time telemetry testing:

$$ \text{NP} = \left( \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N P_{30,i}^4 \right)^{\frac{1}{4}} $$

Where:
*   $\text{TSS}$ and $\text{NP}$ reflect the exponential weighting of training stress, scaling with the 4th power of mechanical power output to match physiological load.
*   $RER$ represents the Respiratory Exchange Ratio, indicating substrate oxidation ratios (carbohydrate vs. fat combustion).
*   $W'_{t}$ represents the instantaneous anaerobic work capacity remaining, measured in Joules (J), which drains non-linearly above FTP and reconstitutes exponentially during recovery.

Here, $P_{30,i}$ is the 30-second rolling average power of the athlete. 
Data align now.

## Step 4: Troubleshooting Sensor Drift
In the field, sensor drift occurs due to mud buildup and environmental sealing degradation. Telemetry casing seals must be checked for water ingress. 
If moisture penetrates the casing, it will short-circuit the strain gauges. This causes erratic power spikes. 
Friction losses from worn chain links or dry pulley wheels can waste up to six Watts. This mechanical loss goes unrecorded by crank-based power meters. 
Check the sensor.
Clean the drivetrain regularly with degreaser. 
Apply a high-quality ceramic chain lube.
This reduces friction losses.
Check for chain stretch using a chain checker tool. 
If the chain wear exceeds 0.5%, replace it immediately. 
By maintaining mechanical tolerances, sports scientists can trust telemetry data. This ensures high-fidelity monitoring of metabolic capacity.
