# Force Vector Alignment in Cleat Lateral Optimization

## Abstract
Locomotor performance requires efficient mechanical power transfer. Cleat lateral stance represents a key variable within this paradigm. This paper investigates frontal-plane force transmission during high-torque pedaling. Joint misalignment triggers patellofemoral strain. We examine these mechanisms. Forces direct locomotion.

## Literature Review
Prior research demonstrates that frontal plane knee tracking correlates with overuse injuries. The literature consensus suggests that misaligned foot placement alters the mechanical loading profile of the patella. By adjusting cleat lateral stance, sports scientists seek to achieve rectilinear movement of the knee joint. Pathological tibial rotation occurs when the lateral position of the foot is incorrect. Empirical validation indicates that a narrow Q-factor increases joint shear force. Legs track crookedly.

A wider lateral offset shifts the loading pattern. This adjustment alters muscular activation. Authors suggest that individual anatomy dictates the ideal foot position on the pedal. Joint stress remains a major concern for endurance athletes. Our study builds on these established kinesiological principles.

## Methodology
To mathematically represent the joint force vectors and leverage associated with **cleat lateral stance**, we apply trigonometric link-node models of the lower limbs:

$$ \theta_{\text{knee}} = \arccos\left( \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab} \right) $$

Where:
*   $L_{\text{saddle}}$ is the saddle height calculated via the Lemond or 109% inseam formulas, serving as the baseline for joint flexion.
*   $\theta_{\text{knee}}$ is the dynamic knee angle, modeled using the cosine rule where $a$, $b$, and $c$ represent the femur length, tibia length, and effective seat height.
*   $F_{\text{joint}}$ represents the shear force acting on the knee joint as a function of the pedaling force and joint extension angles.

To validate our hypothesis testing framework, we monitored fifteen competitive cyclists using infrared motion capture systems operating at 250 Hz. Longitudinal biomechanical assessment protocols tracked knee displacement in the frontal plane under varying lateral offsets. Cyclists pedaled at a constant workload of 300 watts. We recorded torque variation. Foot positioning changed systematically. Our dynamic research focuses on how the lateral placement of the shoe on the pedal body alters the vertical and shear force vectors recorded by biomechanical sensors during high-cadence workloads. Saddle height stabilizes.

We measured knee extension angles between $140^{\circ}$ and $150^{\circ}$ at bottom dead center to establish baseline kinematic consistency. These parameters represent standard operational limits.

## Discussion
Data suggest that lateral cleat positioning influences the frontal plane projection angle of the knee. Optimal stance width minimizes lateral shear force. Knee trajectories stabilize.

We observed statistical significance when adjusting foot position by five millimeters. This adjustment changed joint shear forces. By adjusting the Q-factor configuration of the bicycle frame, sports scientists can realign the mechanical axis of the lower limb to match the individual structural pelvis dimensions of elite athletes. Fitters shift cleats. Torque rises smoothly. Our findings confirm that anatomical variations require personalized lateral adjustments. Methodological limitations include the absence of real-time electromyography. Future research should combine joint kinematics with active muscle activation maps to enhance performance modeling. Locomotor performance optimization remains a high priority for coaching staffs that seek to maximize power output while simultaneously mitigating the long-term risk of tendon overuse injuries. Symmetry protects joints.
